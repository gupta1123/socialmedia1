# SKILL.md — Premium Festival Poster Prompting

## Purpose

Use this skill to generate high-quality prompts for **festival-first social posters** that feel like premium Canva/editorial templates.

This skill is for creating festival images such as:
- Diwali
- Eid
- Navratri / Durga Puja
- Ganesh Chaturthi
- Holi
- Republic Day / Independence Day
- Janmashtami
- Christmas / New Year
- Saraswati Puja
- Ugadi / Gudi Padwa / Vishu / Baisakhi
- other similar festive greeting posters

The goal is **not** to generate raw images, loud greeting cards, or project renders with festive touches.

The goal is to generate prompts that produce:
- premium
- minimal to moderately decorative
- aesthetic
- illustrative or graphic
- well-contained
- social-media-ready festive posters

---

## The 3 core problems this skill must solve

### Problem 1 — Wrong subject
Bad outputs turn into:
- project/building-led festive posts
- real-estate render with some diyas
- architecture as the hero

**Correct behavior:**
Festival posters should be **festival-first**.
The image should usually be led by:
- a symbol
- a silhouette
- a devotional graphic
- a folk illustration
- a ceremonial object
- an invitation-card composition

**Default rule:**
Do **not** include buildings, projects, amenities, entrances, or architecture unless the user explicitly asks for a project-led festive post.

---

### Problem 2 — Wrong taste
Bad outputs become:
- too saturated
- too glowy
- too busy
- too 3D
- too fantasy-like
- too "AI greeting card"
- too loud and painful to the eyes

**Correct behavior:**
Prompts should steer toward:
- premium minimal
- muted or controlled multi-color palettes
- flatter illustration
- soft texture
- clean poster containment
- restrained ornament
- tasteful hierarchy
- editorial / invitation-card / symbolic poster feel

---

### Problem 3 — Too little variety
Bad outputs may become tasteful but repetitive:
- same beige paper background
- same centered icon
- same arch card
- same soft serif feel
- same template for every festival
- same dark luxury card with one metallic accent
- same centered text block with symbolic object underneath
- same dual-tone reduction even when the festival deserves a richer palette

**Correct behavior:**
Maintain quality **and** vary these levers:
- poster archetype
- illustration family
- palette mood
- composition logic
- ornament density
- border/frame treatment
- contrast behavior

Variety should happen **inside good taste**, not by becoming flashy.

---

## Non-negotiable principles

1. **BRIEF OVERRIDES THIS SKILL: If the brief explicitly specifies mood, lighting, style, colors, atmosphere, or visual direction, that ALWAYS takes priority over any preference in this skill.**
2. **Festival-first, not project-first**
3. **Poster-first, not plain image-first**
4. **Premium restraint over loud celebration**
5. **Illustrative / graphic treatment over photoreal festival fantasy**
6. **Controlled multi-color is welcome**
7. **Muted does not mean beige-only**
8. **Use whitespace and containment**
9. **One strong hero idea is better than many competing ideas**
10. **Decorative systems should feel intentional, not crowded**
11. **Every prompt should pick a clear aesthetic family before writing**
12. **Do not let every festival collapse into the same centered premium card**
13. **Do not reduce culturally rich festivals to only one dark background plus one metallic accent unless the brief explicitly asks for that**

---

## What the output should feel like

The prompts generated with this skill should aim for posters that feel like:
- premium Canva template
- editorial festive greeting
- tasteful cultural poster
- soft invitation card
- symbolic devotional print
- illustrated social poster
- premium stationery-like social graphic

They should **not** feel like:
- cheap greeting card
- overdecorated devotional calendar art
- AI fantasy poster
- neon festival explosion
- photoreal cinematic festive ad
- cluttered event flyer

## Prompting workflow

Follow this order before writing any prompt.

### Step 1 — Identify the festival
Choose the festival and its visual vocabulary.

Examples:
- **Diwali:** diya, rangoli, mandala, lamps, warm ceremonial light
- **Eid:** arch, crescent, lanterns, gentle geometry, calm sacred atmosphere
- **Navratri / Durga Puja:** Durga eyes, dandiya, garba silhouettes, arches, devotional symmetry
- **Ganesh Chaturthi:** symbolic Ganesha form, auspicious motifs, soft ceremonial framing
- **Holi:** controlled celebratory figures, color bowls, selective powder treatment, playful movement
- **Republic Day / Independence Day:** Ashoka Chakra, flowing tricolor bands, civic geometry, paper-ribbon movement, emblematic national symbolism, restrained patriotic palette
- **Janmashtami:** Krishna silhouette, flute, peacock-feather motifs
- **Christmas / New Year:** ornaments, wreath-like containment, ribbon-like forms, seasonal greenery, winter-light mood

---

### Step 2 — Choose one poster archetype
Pick **one** archetype per prompt. Do not mix too many.

#### Poster archetypes
- **Central icon poster**
  one strong symbol at center
- **Invitation-card poster**
  framed, contained, airy, elegant
- **Silhouette-led poster**
  one figure or symbolic silhouette
- **Bold graphic devotional poster**
  flatter, stronger shapes, more graphic
- **Folk illustration poster**
  stylized cultural scene or characters
- **Motif-pattern poster**
  decorative system leads the design
- **Minimal symbolic poster**
  very restrained, few elements, strong negative space
- **Typography-forward poster**
  only when explicitly needed and the model is reliable at text
- **Ribbon / band-sweep poster**
  asymmetrical flowing bands or layered paper-ribbon movement
- **Emblem and field poster**
  one emblematic symbol anchored by a shaped color-field system rather than a literal centered card

---

### Step 3 — Choose one illustration family
This is critical for variety.

#### Illustration families
- flat vector-like illustration
- lightly textured editorial illustration
- paper-cut / layered paper style
- block-print inspired graphic style
- line-art devotional style
- silhouette graphic style
- folk-art inspired illustration
- watercolor editorial illustration
- premium minimal symbolic art
- poster-graphic with restrained shading

**Default preference:**
Use flatter or lightly textured styles.
Avoid glossy 3D, photoreal, or hyper-rendered festive art.

---

### Step 4 — Choose a palette mood
**IMPORTANT: If the brief specifies colors, or the brand provides a palette, those take priority. These palette mood examples are defaults when brief doesn't specify.**

Muted does **not** mean beige-only.

Use 3 to 5 coordinated colors.
Favor softened, curated palettes.

#### Palette mood examples

##### A. Dark ceremonial
- deep aubergine
- wine red
- burnt saffron
- antique gold
- ember orange

##### B. Airy editorial
- parchment cream
- dusty teal
- sage grey
- faded olive
- soft bronze

##### C. Earthy spiritual
- terracotta
- cocoa brown
- muted saffron
- deep olive
- warm cream

##### D. Bold but softened
- oxblood
- muted vermilion
- dusty peach
- charcoal
- clay sand

##### E. Playful but controlled
- chalky coral
- faded indigo
- muted turmeric
- dusty mint
- soft plum

##### F. Seasonal premium
- forest green
- muted burgundy
- antique gold
- soft ivory
- brown-black

##### G. Patriotic refined
- muted saffron
- warm ivory
- softened deep green
- indigo / Ashoka navy
- parchment or stone neutral

**Important:**
Multiple colors are good when curated.
Avoid candy-bright, neon, or "all colors at once."
4 to 5 colors are allowed when they are softened and well-controlled.
Do not collapse a festive or patriotic poster into dual-tone only unless the brief explicitly asks for a monochrome or two-tone route.

---

### Step 5 — Choose composition logic
Choose one clear structure.

#### Composition options
- symmetrical ceremonial vertical
- centered emblem / medallion
- arch-contained invitation card
- silhouette with open space
- lower-plaque + central hero
- top-title + central motif + footer band
- framed poster with corner ornaments
- airy editorial card with lots of whitespace
- asymmetrical ribbon sweep
- off-center emblem with counterbalanced text field
- layered color-field poster

**Important:**
- Do not default every prompt to a centered emblem on a plain dark background.
- Do not make every route feel like the same centered premium greeting card.

---

### Step 6 — Choose decorative density
This controls taste.

#### Decorative density levels
- **Sparse** — minimal, premium, quiet
- **Balanced** — enough detail to feel festive
- **Moderate** — richer but still controlled
- **Rich but contained** — only for some festivals, never chaotic

**Default:** Sparse or Balanced

Do not let the prompt drift into:
- confetti everywhere
- sparkles everywhere
- particles everywhere
- heavy pattern on every inch
- too many festival objects at once

---

### Step 7 — Choose text containment style
Even when exact text is not specified, the design should support poster hierarchy.

#### Text containment styles
- top title zone
- lower greeting plaque
- central card field
- airy upper header space
- framed invitation panel
- small footer sign-off zone
- centered title above hero symbol

Do not force exact x/y positions unless needed.
Do ensure the poster has a natural space for:
- title
- greeting
- support line
- small footer/sign-off if needed

---

## Brand integration rules

If the poster is for a brand account:
- let the brand influence **taste**, not the subject
- keep the festival as the hero
- keep brand presence subtle
- do not force a building into the poster
- do not turn it into a project ad
- **NEVER mention the brand name in the prompt** - do not say "Brand Name's colors" or "the Brand Name logo" - use "brand colors" or "the logo" instead
- Use brand colors via hex codes (#XXXXXX) but do not name the brand

Good brand influence can appear as:
- premium restraint
- cleaner composition
- better spacing
- softer palette choices
- more elegant border/frame logic
- subtle footer sign-off
- disciplined decorative density
- brand colors applied subtly via hex codes
- logo placement described as "monochromatic knockout" or "transparent watermark"

## Prompt writing rules

### Always do
- name the festival
- choose a clear hero symbol or hero visual idea
- choose one poster archetype
- choose one illustration family
- choose one composition logic
- specify the palette mood
- specify restraint and decorative density
- specify the desired finish: premium, illustrative, editorial, template-like

### Usually do
- mention "poster" not just "image"
- mention "designed social poster" or "template-like composition"
- mention "flat or lightly textured illustration"
- mention "breathing room" or "negative space"
- mention "contained composition" or "clear poster hierarchy"

### Avoid doing
- forcing buildings or project visuals by default
- describing too many unrelated objects
- asking for rich festive energy without restraint
- using vague words like "vibrant" unless controlled
- leaving the palette behavior undefined
- mixing 3–4 archetypes in one prompt
- describing glow, sparkle, fantasy light, cinematic dust, or dramatic particles

---

## Default prompt template

Use this template and adapt carefully.

```text
Create a premium [festival name] festive poster in a [illustration family] style. Build the composition as a [poster archetype] with [hero symbol / hero visual idea] as the main focal point. The design should feel like a tasteful Canva editorial template or refined festive greeting poster rather than a loud greeting card or plain decorative image.

Use a [composition logic] with [decorative density] ornament, clear poster containment, and enough breathing room for the design to feel elegant and complete. The visual language should feel [mood words: calm / ceremonial / playful but controlled / devotional / airy / spiritual / refined], with culturally recognisable but modern design treatment.

Use a curated palette built from [3–5 colors or palette mood], keeping the tones softened, premium, and controlled rather than flashy. The illustration should feel flat or lightly textured rather than photoreal, glossy, or hyper-rendered. Decorative elements should support the composition, not crowd it.

The result should feel premium, aesthetic, illustrative, and social-media-ready, with clear hierarchy and a polished poster finish.

Avoid hyper-saturated colors, neon accents, fantasy glow, crowded ornament, photoreal rendering, kitschy greeting-card styling, and chaotic visual energy.
```
